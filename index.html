Introduction

AP and IB programs are a major gateway to college success and significantly contribute to student outcomes by providing an avenue for students to demonstrate college-level rigor before leaving secondary education. Students who can achieve high marks in these exams receive a head start on their collegiate careers while also strengthening both their college applications as well as scholarships opportunities.

New York State, home to one of the nation's largest and most diverse public education systems, has significant disparities in both access to and success within AP/IB programs. While some districts boast robust advanced coursework offerings and high achievement rates, others struggle to provide students with even basic access to these college-preparatory opportunities. These gaps often mirror broader patterns of educational inequity, disproportionately affecting students in under-resourced districts and those from economically disadvantaged backgrounds.

This project seeks to answer three central questions:

  1. What patterns exist in AP/IB performance across New York school districts? Can we identify natural groupings of districts based on exam performance and demographic characteristics?

  2. Which districts and student populations face the greatest opportunity gaps? Are certain regions or demographic groups systematically underserved in access to advanced coursework (less exams offered)?

  3. What actionable insights can inform policy decisions? How can data-driven analysis help policymakers and education leaders allocate resources more effectively to support struggling districts?

Why does this matter?

Educational equity has profound implications for economic opportunity and social mobility. Students without access to rigorous coursework are at a significant disadvantage in college admissions and are challenged with less scholarship aid once enrolled. By identifying which districts and populations are falling behind, we can provide evidence-based recommendations for targeted interventions, resource allocation, and policy reforms. Understanding these patterns is essential to ensuring that every student, regardless of zip code or socioeconomic status, has the opportunity to reach their full academic potential. The opportunity of rigorous education should not be restricted to only those

Using comprehensive AP/IB examination data from the 2023-2024 school year provided by the New York State Education Department, this analysis will employ data science techniques to uncover systemic patterns, identify at-risk districts, and ultimately contribute to more equitable educational outcomes across the state.

Data Curation

The dataset we will analyze for this project is the Advanced Placement (AP) and International Baccalaureate (IB) dataset (https://data.nysed.gov/files/apib/2324/APIB24.zip), specifically AP_IB_Assessment_2024.mdb that was subsequently converted to a .csv using Microsoft Excel that was published by the New York State Education Department in 2024 (https://data.nysed.gov/downloads.php). The primary information contained in this data set are the AP and IB test scores recorded from the 2023-2024 school year in New York State. The dataset gives a full breakdown of student AP and IB exam performance, organized in multiple layers so we can analyze results from broad statewide patterns down to very specific student groups. It includes:

Geographic Aggregation Levels

Data is reported at several levels:

  • Statewide
  • New York City (NYC)
  • County
  • School district
  • Needs/Resource categories (e.g., high-need districts, average-need, etc.)

This lets us compare performance across regions and district types.

Student Subgroups

Within every geographic level, results are divided into detailed demographic and educational subgroups, including:

  • Gender: Male, Female, Nonbinary
  • Race/Ethnicity: Hispanic, White, Black, Multiracial, American Indian, Asian/Pacific Islander
  • Program: general education, English language learner (ELL vs non-ELL)
  • Household status: economically disadvantaged, migrant, homeless, foster care, students with a parent in the armed forces (and their not counterparts)

This structure allows us to explore equity, opportunity gaps, and subgroup performance patterns.

Exam-Level Details

For each subgroup, the dataset provides:

  • Exam Name: (AP Bio, AP Calculus AB, IB Chem, etc)
  • Student grade level: (9-12)
  • Score Distributions:
    • AP: number of students receiving 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5
    • IB: number receiving 1 through 7

Interpreting the Data

The dataset is broken down by geographic region subgroup exam grade score, repeated hundreds of thousands of times so every student who has tested in the state of NY is present once in each aggregate. Its designed to let us analyze performance deeplyfrom statewide trends all the way down to the performance of specific subgroups in particular districts, grades, and exams. It provides both participation counts and score distributions, giving us everything we need to study achievement patterns and equity outcomes.

Let's return to those NaN values in the numeric columns from above. These are missing data. Looking at the dataframe and its documentation, it can be seen that rows with less than 5 students tested have been excluded for privacy reasons. This is a MNAR (Missing Not At Random) missing data problem because the probability of data being missing is related to the unobserved values themselves. Because we cannot observe the scores for these small groups, and because their group size is likely correlated with their performance, the missingness mechanism is informative about the missing values themselves, making this a case of MNAR. For more information about missing data and how to handle it, consider reading the following article: https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/machine-learning/types-of-missing-data-in-machine-learning/

# Define AP proficiency
ap_proficient_levels = ["level3_cnt", "level4_cnt", "level5_cnt", "level6_cnt", "level7_cnt"]
# Define IB proficiency
ib_proficient_levels = ["level4_cnt", "level5_cnt", "level6_cnt", "level7_cnt"]
# Define a function to sum the proficient columns per each row and return this value
def compute_proficient(row):
    if row["APIB_indicator"] == "AP":
        return sum([row[col] for col in ap_proficient_levels])
    elif row["APIB_indicator"] == "IB":
        return sum([row[col] for col in ib_proficient_levels])
    else:
        return pd.NA
# Call to our defined function which stores the summed proficiency in our new column proficient_student_cnt
df["proficient_student_cnt"] = df.apply(compute_proficient, axis=1)

Exploratory Analysis 1: Proficiency Rates by County

First, we will look at Proficiency Scores by County across New York. Our goal is to identify which counties are underperforming and by how much, so that targeted support can be provided.

Hypothesis:

  • H: There is no significant difference in the AP/IB proficiency rates between counties.

  • H: There is a significant difference in the AP/IB proficiency rates between counties.

alpha level: 0.05

Dataset Characteristics:

The dataset contains entries across 63 counties, with two key features analyzed: proficient_student_cnt and tested_student_cnt. We will do two things, show a visualization of proficient students per county by plotting the proficiency rate (number of proficient students / number of tested students * 100) and perform a chi squared test in order to determine the significance of the relationship between counties and proficiency. In order to do this we will aggregate the proficient students per count and create a separate column "not_proficient" which is the number of tested students - proficient students aggregated per county. This will allow us to perform a chi squared test on two categorical variables.

For more information on chi-squared test, consider visiting the following site: https://www.jmp.com/en/statistics-knowledge-portal/chi-square-test

# Imports

Exploratory Analysis 2: Proficiency Rates by Race/Ethnicity

Next, we will look at Proficiency Rate by Race/Ethnicity across New York. Our goal with this analysis is to determine whether or not demographics are an indicator of performance in AP/IB testing. This addresses the question of an opportunity gap, and seeing as there is a statistical significance on geography's influence on proficiency rate from our first analysis, this can help determine what groups within those schools need more support.

Hypothesis:

  • H: There is no significant difference in AP/IB proficiency rates between different racial/ethnic student subgroups.

  • H: There is a significant difference in AP/IB proficiency rates between at least two racial/ethnic student subgroups.

alpha level: 0.05

Dataset Characteristics:

The original dataset contains a subgroup_name column that includes various demographic and socieoeconomic categories (e.g., "All Students", "Female", "Hispanic", "Economically Disadvantaged"). To perform a valid analysis, we first need to filter the data to isolate only the racial and ethnic subgroups, removing aggregate categories like "All Students" or gender-based groups to avoid double-counting.

These groups are:

  • American Indian/Alaska Native
  • Black
  • Hispanic
  • Asian/Pacific Islander
  • White
  • Multiracial

We chose a One-way ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) for this analysis as we are comparing a continuous variable (proficiency_rate) across more than two independent categorical groups (racial/ethnic subgroups). This statistical test allows us to determine if the variation between the group means is statistically significant compared to the variation within the groups. We will also create a box plot showing the AP/IB Proficiency Rates by Racial/Ethnic groups.

Consider visiting the following site for more information on the One-way ANOVA: https://www.jmp.com/en/statistics-knowledge-portal/one-way-anova

In[19]:
# Filter by Racial/Ethnic Subgroups ONLY
filtered_subgroups = [
    'American Indian/Alaska Native',
    'Black',
    'Hispanic',
    'Asian/Pacific Islander',
    'White',
    'Multiracial'
]

# Create a subset dataframe copy filtered by race/ethnicity
df_race = df[df['subgroup_name'].isin(filtered_subgroups)].copy()

# Organize and group data by race/ethnicity subgroups

f_stat, p_val = stats.f_oneway(*groups)

Results:

After performing a One-Way ANOVA test, we found a p-value of 0.00 (p < 0.001), significantly less than the alpha level of 0.05, which means we reject the null hypothesis and conclude it is statistically significant that there is a significant difference in AP/IB Proficiency Rates between Racial/Ethnic Subgroups. With a massive F-statistic of 1591.51, we see a profound disparity between racial demographics.

Insights:

We see that a student's racial/ethnic background is a statistically significant factor in their likelihood of achieving proficiency on AP/IB exams, validating the concerns of opportunity gaps in our introduction. These gaps are very real and statistically undeniable; and shows that demographic factors also highly influence student performance, not just geography. While it is important to identify what areas in the state are underserved, as property taxes influence school funding, it is also important to determine who within those school populations are in need of more resources. There is a great disparity between Asians/White/Multiracial students and American Indian/Black/Hispanic students, who are historically underserved.

Exploratory Analysis 3: Proficiency Rates by Need/Resource Category

Finally, we look at Proficiency by Need/Resource Category

Hypothesis:

  • H: Student proficiency rates are independent of need/resource category.
  • H: Student proficiency rates depend on need/resource category.

Alpha level: 0.05

Dataset Characteristics:

The dataset contains 467,535 entries across 7 NRC (Need/Resource Category) categories, with two key features analyzed: proficient_student_cnt and non_proficient_cnt (subtracted from tested_student_cnt). The NRC categories represent different school resource levels, ranging from low-need to high-need districts.

However, certain NRC categories are over-represented in terms of total student counts, which could influence the statistical power of the chi-square test. The chi-square test was chosen as one of our analysis techniques because...

  • We are comparing categorical data (NRC categories) against count data (proficient vs non-proficient students)
  • We want to test for independence between two categorical variables
  • The sample sizes are sufficiently large for chi-square assumptions

Primary Analysis

From our exploratory data analysis we know there is significant impact that counties (made up of many districts) and racial demographics have on proficiency. An easy extension of this is to see how districts may be grouped into groups in order to determine which school districts need support for their progrmams.

We will be using the machine learning technique K-means clustering to identify patterns across New York school districts based on AP/IB exam performance. This choice is driven by the nature of our research questions and the structure of our dataset, unlike classification or regression tasks where we would predict a known outcome variable, our objective is to discover natural groupings within the data that may not be immediately obvious. We want to understand how districts naturally group together based on multiple performance and demographic factors simultaneously. K-means clustering is specifically designed for this type of unsupervised learning problemfinding structure in data without pre-defined labels.

Each school district can be categorized into numerous features:

  • Performance metrics: Avg AP/IB scores, percentage of students receiving passing (college qualified) scores (AP >= 3, IB >= 4)

  • Access metrics: number of different exams offered

  • Demographic factors: percentage of economically disadvantaged, racial/ethnic diversity, household situation (homeless, single parent)

  • General Resource indicators: Need Resource Category

By grouping districts into clusters, we can identify distinct profiles that can help us answer our proposed questions:

  • High-performing, high-access districts may serve as models for best practices
  • High-potential districts (high-access but lower scores) might benefit from teacher training and curriculum support
  • Low-access districts (minimal AP/IB offerings) require investment in program infrastructure
  • High-need districts (low performance across metrics) may need comprehensive support including funding, staffing, and resources

These cluster-based insights are more actionable than simple rankings or regression charts because they group districts with similar challenges, enabling policymakers to design interventions that address systemic issues shared by multiple communities. It will allow us to visualize where resources are lacking and which districts need more support.

Once districts are clustered, we can perform further analysis by examining demographic subgroup performance within each cluster. Some additional questions we can look at answering are:

  • Do economically disadvantaged students perform differently in high-performing versus low-performing clusters?
  • Are certain racial/ethnic groups underrepresented in AP/IB participation across all clusters, or is this issue concentrated in specific district types?
  • Which clusters show the largest achievement gaps between demographic subgroups?

Our plan to implement K-means analysis will include multiple steps resulting in many plots to help us understand our data better.

  • Feature engineering: first, we need to aggregate our data by district, keeping features from the exam-level data such as scores, demographic compositions, and NRC.
  • Feature Scaling: Next, we standardize all features to ensure the variables with larger ranges don't dominate the clustering.
    • AP scores go up to 5, while IB goes up to 7 as while proficiency rate is a percentage 1 to 100.
  • Selection of K hyperparameter: Since the goal of our research is to identify three groups (high, mid, and low performing) the elbow method is not necesary and we will set k to 3 to reflect this goal.
  • Cluster Validation: Did we select the right clusters? Examine the cluster characteristics to ensure they are meaningful to providing insight into answering our questions

Proficiency rate

We begin by importing the necessary tools from scikit-learn: StandardScaler for feature normalization and KMeans for clustering. Since our raw dataset contains individual exam records (one row per exam, grade, subgroup, etc), we aggregate all results to the district level by grouping by district_name and summing the total proficient and tested student counts across all exams.

We then calculate the proficiency rate--the percentage of students achieving college-credit scores (AP >= 3, IB >= 4)--which serves as our primary performance metric. This normalized metric allows fair comparison between large and small districts regardless of total exam volume.

In[21]:
# Imports
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

# Add these features to your aggregation:
X = df.groupby('district_name').agg({
    'proficient_student_cnt': 'sum',
    'tested_student_cnt': 'sum',
    'test_name': 'nunique',  # Number of different exams offered (ACCESS)
    'nrc_desc': 'first',  # Needs/Resource category
    'county_name': 'first'
}).reset_index()

X['proficiency_rate'] = X['proficient_student_cnt'] / X['tested_student_cnt'] * 100
X['num_exams_offered'] = X['test_name']  # Rename for clarity
X['avg_students_per_exam'] = X['tested_student_cnt'] / X['num_exams_offered']

# Selection of features:
features = X[['proficiency_rate', 'num_exams_offered', 'avg_students_per_exam']]

We then standardize the proficiency rate using StandardScaler, which transforms the data to have mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1. This is essential for K-means because the algorithm uses distance calculations; without standardization, features with larger numerical ranges (like IB scores, which range 1-7) would dominate the clustering process.

We then apply K-means with k=3 clusters to group districts into three performance tiers: high-performing, mid-performing, and low-performing. The algorithm iteratively assigns districts to clusters based on similarity of their proficiency rates. Setting random_state=42 ensures our results are reproducible across multiple runs.

The resulting cluster assignments are added back to our district dataframe, allowing us to analyze cluster characteristics, examine geographic patterns, and investigate demographic factors associated with each performance tier.

Insights and Conclusion

Through this tutorial, we aimed to uncover the systemic patterns defining AP and IB performance in New York State. By analyzing over 250,000 aggregated exam records from the 2023-2024 school year, we were able to sucessfully yield actionable policy from raw data. Overall, our exploratory data analysis established that student success in AP/IB courses is not solely dependent on individual effort, but is systematically influenced by external, structural factors such as geography, demographics, and financial stances.

Through a Chi-Square test analysis, we confirmed both county location and the need/resource category of a school district are highly significant factors in student proficiency, as we saw $p < 0.001$ for both Chi-Square tests. This confirms that money is a very strong lever for student improvement, as areas with low needs and high resources consistently outperform high-need areas. Furthermore, through a One-Way ANOVA test, we found a statistically significant relationship between a student's racial background and AP/IB proficiency rates ($p < 0.001$). This also confirms our concerns about opportunity gaps in our second question, displaying how certain student populations are systematically underserved.

We also saw that NY school districts fall into three clear performance tiers (Low, Mid, High) using K-Means clustering, answering our first question on what patters we see in our dataset. This allows us to find more targeted, rather than generic, state intervention. To answer our third question on what these insights can do to influence policy decisions: First we want to preface that while our conclusion is data-driven, there is no "one-size-fits-all" solution. By leveraging this data, policymakers can ensure that resources are directed where the need is greatest so that the impact on students and the reduction of opportunity gaps will be most effective and significant.

From our analysis, we can conclude that for the Low-Performing cluster, policy effort must prioritize allocating more funding and improving more access to better educational resources, as these districts require more comprehensive support to build foundational advanced coursework infrastucture and equity programs. We can also conclude that for the Mid-Performing cluster, the policy effort should shift to optimizing better support such as more effective teacher training and curriculum reform similar to the High-Performing cluster to raise proficiency rates. As we have also identified demographic gaps in opportunity from the ANOVA, targeted funding should account for said demographic gaps, ensuring resources are directed not just to "low/mid-performing" schools generally, but to the specific student subgroups that we have identified are being systematically underserved.
